# Renewable energy responses to the 2026 Iran war energy crisis

The US-Israeli military strikes on Iran ("Operation Epic Fury," February 28, 2026) and Iran's subsequent closure of the Strait of Hormuz have triggered what the IEA calls the "greatest global energy security challenge in history" — disrupting **~20% of global oil and 19% of LNG trade**, sending Brent crude above $110/barrel and doubling Asian LNG spot prices. Countries with high existing renewable energy penetration — particularly Spain, Pakistan, and the Nordic states — are demonstrably weathering the crisis better, while the shock has catalyzed the most significant wave of clean energy policy announcements since the 2022 Russian gas crisis. The March 2026 responses split sharply into two categories: near-term emergency conservation (rationing, work-from-home mandates, business curfews) in vulnerable developing countries, and structural RE acceleration (auction fast-tracking, new GW targets, building mandates, tax incentives) in countries with stronger institutional capacity. This report compiles every identifiable renewable energy, efficiency, and electrification policy response from March 1–31, 2026.

## Spain's RE-heavy grid proved its worth as the crisis shield

Spain stands out as the clearest vindication of long-term renewable energy investment. Having doubled wind and solar capacity since 2019 (adding over 40 GW), Spain entered the crisis generating **over 60% of its electricity from renewables**. The results are striking: wholesale electricity prices remained at €37–57/MWh in Spain while climbing to €113/MWh in Germany and €141/MWh in Italy. The difference lies in marginal pricing mechanics — gas set Spain's electricity price only **15% of the time**, compared to **89% in Italy**. Strong winds and heavy rains in early 2026 produced record hydroelectric and wind output, further cushioning the blow. PM Sánchez pointed out that on a recent Saturday, electricity was nearly free.

Spain's €5 billion crisis package went beyond emergency subsidies. Embedded within it were structural clean energy incentives: "massive" income tax deductions for household installation of rooftop solar, heat pumps, and EV charging points, plus accelerated permitting for renewable energy projects. The package explicitly framed these not as climate measures but as investments in "energy sovereignty." Portugal and the Nordic countries showed similar patterns — all registering the lowest gas-driven electricity prices in the EU throughout March, confirming that high RE penetration is now the single strongest predictor of energy price resilience.

## Pakistan's accidental solar revolution became a lifeline

Pakistan's story is perhaps the most remarkable. The country's **51 GW of imported solar panels** — almost entirely a bottom-up, consumer-led phenomenon enabled by zero tariffs on Chinese panels from 2013 to 2025 — now provides 20–25% of total electricity and has avoided an estimated **$12 billion in fossil fuel imports since 2020**, with an additional $6.3 billion projected for 2026. Solar panel prices fell fivefold between 2022 and 2024, making rooftop installations accessible even in lower-income households. This unplanned solar boom now serves as Pakistan's primary energy security buffer.

Despite this structural advantage, Pakistan's government response was overwhelmingly focused on demand reduction: a four-day government workweek, 50% work-from-home, two-week school closures, grounding 60% of official vehicles, and the largest fuel price hike in the country's history (20%). Digital fuel rationing was introduced by late March. Controversially, Pakistan's energy regulator (NEPRA) had just weakened net-metering incentives in February 2026, shifting from 1:1 net metering to net billing — a move critics argue undermines the very distributed solar growth providing the country's strongest crisis buffer. With only 25–28 days of fuel reserves, Pakistan's experience powerfully illustrates both the protective value of RE adoption and the policy contradictions that persist even amid crisis.

## Cuba, the Philippines, and the sharpest edges of vulnerability

Cuba faces a unique double crisis: the broader Iran-related disruption layered atop a separate US oil blockade imposed in January 2026 after the Venezuela intervention. The island's **34 solar parks (560 MW peak capacity)** now generate roughly 38% of daytime electricity — a remarkable figure given that solar contributed less than 5% just a few years ago. President Díaz-Canel confirmed the country was operating on "solar power, natural gas, and thermoelectric plants." But analysts warn this is "too little, too late" without battery storage for nighttime demand. An island-wide blackout lasting over 29 hours on March 16 underscored the fragility. Cuba's grid operates at only 34% capacity, and the government's 24% RE target by 2030 requires $8–10 billion in investment the country does not have.

The Philippines became **the first country to declare a national energy emergency** on March 24, importing 98% of its oil from the Middle East and holding only 45 days of reserves. Measures included a four-day government workweek, computers off during lunch, AC no lower than 24°C, and promotion of rooftop solar with battery storage for diesel-dependent areas. The Institute for Climate and Sustainable Cities began installing solar on government buildings and hospitals. Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, and Myanmar imposed some of the most extreme rationing: Sri Lanka declared every Wednesday a public holiday, Myanmar introduced odd-even driving schemes, and Bangladesh closed universities and imposed 9pm business curfews.

## The electrification surge happened at the kitchen table

One of the most striking findings is India's rapid, crisis-driven shift to electric cooking. With LPG prices spiking after 21 ships stalled in the Gulf and domestic refill waits extending to 25 days, **electric cooking became roughly 20% cheaper than LPG**. The city of Gurugram saw electricity consumption jump by 8 million units in just 9 days as households and businesses switched to induction stoves. Andhra Pradesh began deploying induction stoves in government childcare centers. IRCTC directed railway catering units to switch to induction. Street food vendors in Indore's famous Chappan Dukan market swapped gas burners for electric appliances. A state government announced a ₹2/unit electricity subsidy for eateries switching to induction. Analysts estimate electric and biogas cooking could reduce India's LPG demand by 50% by 2050, potentially the most consequential long-term shift catalyzed by this crisis.

Transport electrification saw surging consumer interest but limited new government support. US gas prices rose 27% to $4.29/gallon, driving a **20% spike in EV searches** and a jump in electrified vehicle shopping from 20.7% to 22.4% — but the Trump administration had already repealed EV subsidies. In Europe, Denmark saw used EV searches rise 80%, France's Aramisauto saw EV share nearly double to 12.7%, and Germany reported a 40% jump in EV inquiries. Ethiopia's prior ban on gasoline/diesel car imports (2024) proved prescient, with EVs growing from 1% to 6% of the market and **95% renewable electricity** from hydropower insulating the economy. Nepal's 70%+ EV share of new car sales provided similar protection. Laos was the most notable new mover, slashing EV service fees and ordering government agencies to stop purchasing gasoline vehicles.

## Structural policy acceleration in Europe and South Korea set the pace

Beyond emergency measures, several governments used the crisis to accelerate structural clean energy policy. **Germany** announced an additional 12 GW of onshore wind auction volumes through 2030 (approximately 2,000 extra turbines), projecting savings of €1 billion per year on gas imports and 6.5 million tonnes of avoided CO2 emissions. Energy Minister Reiche declared "energy policy is now security policy." The **UK** fast-tracked its AR8 renewable auction to July 2026, with up to 18 offshore wind farms eligible, building on a record 14.7 GW secured in prior auction rounds. Energy Secretary Miliband accelerated the Future Homes Standard, mandating solar panels and heat pumps on all new homes in England from 2028. The UK Climate Change Committee published a report finding the total cost of reaching net zero is "no greater than the cost of a single fossil fuel price shock" — and that a net-zero-compliant UK would see household bills rise just 4% during a crisis versus 59% without.

**South Korea** emerged as the most explicitly RE-positive responder among developed Asian economies. President Lee Jae-myung called the crisis "a good opportunity to swiftly and extensively transition to renewable energy," setting a **100 GW RE target by 2030** and pushing legislative amendments to remove local restrictions on solar deployment. A grid innovation plan was ordered within six months. This represented a dramatic reversal from the previous Yoon administration's anti-RE posture. The country simultaneously raised nuclear utilization to 80%+ and proposed a ₩26.2 trillion ($17.1 billion) supplementary budget. The EU launched consultations on its post-2030 renewable energy and energy efficiency frameworks on March 20, with legislative proposals due by year-end — positioning this crisis as the political catalyst for the next generation of European energy targets.

## The US paradox and what the multilateral system actually delivered

The United States presents the starkest paradox: the country that initiated the military action simultaneously pursued policies undermining energy security through clean energy. The Trump administration continued halting all five US offshore wind projects, paid TotalEnergies approximately $1 billion to abandon two wind leases and redirect funds to fossil fuels, and had already truncated IRA wind and solar credits (53–59% projected reduction in new clean power capacity over the next decade, per Rhodium Group). At the same time, some pro-solar signals emerged — Interior began reviewing ~20 stuck solar projects, and several MAGA-aligned figures publicly endorsed rooftop solar. The federal residential clean energy tax credit (30%) was eliminated effective January 1, 2026, leaving state programs as the primary incentive vehicle during the worst energy crisis in decades.

Multilateral institutions responded with unprecedented coordination but limited RE-specific action. The IEA released a record **400 million barrels** from strategic reserves and launched a 2026 Energy Crisis Policy Response Tracker. UNFCCC Executive Secretary Simon Stiell delivered the strongest institutional statement, declaring "fossil fuel dependency is ripping away national security and sovereignty" and noting that "sunlight doesn't depend on narrow and vulnerable shipping straits." The G7 held its first-ever combined finance-energy-central bank meeting since 1975 on March 30 but produced "few actionable outcomes." ASEAN convened a special foreign ministers meeting explicitly calling for "redoubling efforts to switch from fossil fuels to renewables." The IMF began running country-by-country stress tests but announced no specific RE financing. The Green Climate Fund, AfDB, and ADB made no specific crisis-response RE announcements during March.

## Conclusion: the crisis accelerates a divergence already underway

Three patterns emerge clearly from the first month of policy responses. First, **countries with pre-existing high RE penetration are measurably better off** — Spain, Pakistan, Ethiopia, Norway, and the Nordics demonstrate that the energy transition is no longer a future promise but a present-day economic shield, with price differentials of 2–4× between high-RE and high-gas-dependent economies. Second, **the Global South response is overwhelmingly conservation, not acceleration** — school closures, business curfews, rationing, and work-from-home mandates dominate, because structural RE deployment takes time and capital that crisis-hit developing countries cannot mobilize in weeks. Third, **the crisis has opened a new policy window in Europe and South Korea** that may prove more consequential than 2022's Russian gas shock, with Germany's 12 GW wind commitment, the UK's building mandate acceleration, and South Korea's 100 GW target representing concrete, binding commitments rather than aspirational rhetoric.

The deepest insight may be India's kitchen-table revolution. When LPG becomes scarce and expensive, millions of households switch to electric induction cooking within days — not because of policy mandates but because the economics flip decisively. This bottom-up electrification pattern, visible also in Pakistan's solar boom and the global EV interest surge, suggests that price signals from fossil fuel crises now translate into clean energy adoption faster than any previous energy shock — because the alternatives finally exist at competitive cost. As Ember's Sam Butler-Sloss put it: "In the old fossil fuel world, energy security meant diversifying fuel supply. With electrotech, nations now have the tools to increasingly eliminate imported fuels altogether."
